India's judicial reform debate is organised around the queue: pendency counts, disposal rates, judge-to-population ratios. This paper argues that the queue is the second question. The first is whether the room exists — whether the forum a statute promises has been constituted, staffed, and given an unambiguous place in the appellate lattice. We introduce the Judiciary Entity Map (JEM), an open, source-linked structural dataset of India's judicial and quasi-judicial ecosystem: **1,145 entities** and **1,810 relationships** in release v1.0.0, spanning constitutional courts, tribunals, regulators, consumer commissions, ADR bodies, and the appointment, funding, audit, and complaint chains that connect them. Every relationship is grounded, where possible, in constitutional text, statute, gazette notification, or official report, with explicit data-quality flags where it is not. From the dataset we derive a four-part typology of carrying-capacity failure — forums *delayed* (GSTAT, eight years from statute to seated members), forums *never built* (the Arbitration Council of India, the Mediation Council of India, seven state and UT Lokayuktas), forums *hollowed* (APTEL's technical-member vacancy filled only after judicial prompting; Allahabad High Court at roughly half strength), and forums *ambiguous* (the rerouting of Debts Recovery Tribunal appeals from Hyderabad and Visakhapatnam to an appellate tribunal 1,500 kilometres away, generating writ litigation in two High Courts about where to litigate). We show that each failure mode redirects disputes rather than resolving them, and that the redirection lands, predictably, on the constitutional courts. The dataset, its derivation pipeline, and its known gaps are public, versioned, and offered as infrastructure for research the author cannot do alone.

**Keywords:** judicial administration, tribunals, state capacity, institutional design, pendency, India, open data

## Abstract

India's judicial reform debate is organised around the queue: pendency counts, disposal rates, judge-to-population ratios. This paper argues that the queue is the second question. The first is whether the room exists — whether the forum a statute promises has been constituted, staffed, and given an unambiguous place in the appellate lattice. We introduce the Judiciary Entity Map (JEM), an open, source-linked structural dataset of India's judicial and quasi-judicial ecosystem: **1,145 entities** and **1,835 relationships** in release v1.0.0, spanning constitutional courts, tribunals, regulators, consumer commissions, ADR bodies, and the appointment, funding, audit, and complaint chains that connect them. Every relationship is grounded, where possible, in constitutional text, statute, gazette notification, or official report, with explicit data-quality flags where it is not. From the dataset we derive a four-part typology of carrying-capacity failure — forums *delayed* (GSTAT, eight years from statute to seated members), forums *never built* (the Arbitration Council of India, the Mediation Council of India, seven state and UT Lokayuktas), forums *hollowed* (APTEL's technical-member vacancy filled only after judicial prompting; Allahabad High Court at roughly half strength), and forums *ambiguous* (the rerouting of Debts Recovery Tribunal appeals from Hyderabad and Visakhapatnam to an appellate tribunal 1,500 kilometres away, generating writ litigation in two High Courts about where to litigate). We show that each failure mode redirects disputes rather than resolving them, and that the redirection lands, predictably, on the constitutional courts. The dataset, its derivation pipeline, and its known gaps are public, versioned, and offered as infrastructure for research the author cannot do alone.

**Keywords:** judicial administration, tribunals, state capacity, institutional design, pendency, India, open data

## I. The problem: we count the queue, not the room

Ask what ails India's courts and the answer arrives in a familiar unit: cases. Some fifty million pending across tiers, disposal rates, vacancy percentages, the arithmetic of delay. The pendency literature is mature, quantitatively serious, and indispensable. It is also incomplete in a specific way: it treats the set of forums as given and studies the flow through them. The forum itself — whether it exists, whether it is staffed, whether a litigant can tell which one has jurisdiction — is background.

That background is not stable. India's judicial ecosystem runs to something like 1,500 distinct institutions: constitutional courts, subordinate court lattices, central and state tribunals, quasi-judicial regulators, consumer commissions, ombudsmen, arbitration and mediation bodies, and the audit, training, security, and digital-infrastructure organs that keep them running. These institutions are created, merged, abolished, left unconstituted, and rewired by gazette notification with some regularity. The information describing this architecture exists — but scattered across constitutional text, central and state Acts, Department of Justice vacancy reports, tribunal annual reports, CAG audit chains, and individual gazette entries. No single navigable, source-linked map of the administrative architecture existed in the open.

The consequence is a systematic blind spot. A tribunal that is legislated but not constituted does not appear in pendency statistics, because it has no docket. Its caseload appears somewhere else — typically as Article 226 writ petitions in High Courts — where it is counted as High Court pendency and diagnosed, wrongly, as a High Court problem. A tribunal whose appellate routing is ambiguous generates satellite litigation about jurisdiction that is booked as ordinary case volume. In both situations the queue statistics are accurate and the diagnosis they suggest is not. You cannot fix what you cannot see, and what we cannot currently see is **carrying capacity**: the ecosystem's structural ability to receive, hear, and dispose of the disputes the law channels toward it.

This paper does two things. First, it introduces an instrument: the Judiciary Entity Map (JEM), an open structural dataset of the Indian judicial ecosystem, built to make carrying capacity observable, queryable, and citable. Second, it uses the instrument to propose a typology. Working through the dataset's documented structural gaps, four distinct failure modes recur — a forum can be *delayed*, *never built*, *hollowed*, or *ambiguous* — and each mode has a characteristic spillover signature. The typology matters because the four modes have different causes and demand different remedies, yet all four surface in the statistics as the same undifferentiated thing: pendency.

## II. Evidence architecture: what JEM is and how it is built

JEM is a versioned knowledge graph. Release v1.0.0 (July 2026) contains **1,145 entities** and **1,835 relationships**, with **zero orphan nodes** — every entity is wired into the graph through at least one sourced relationship.¹ Entities are YAML records validated against a typed schema: twenty-six controlled entity types (from `ConstitutionalCourt` and `HighCourtBench` through `CentralTribunal`, `RegulatoryBodyQJ`, and `StatutoryBodyNotConstituted`), eleven clusters, and controlled vocabularies for operational status, funding source, audit type, and data quality. Relationships are typed edges across eleven categories — appointment (`Nominates`, `Recommends`, `FormallyAppoints`, `CanRemove`), funding (`PrimaryFunder`, `BudgetAllocatedBy`), appellate chains (`AppealableTo`, `FinalAppealTo`), supervision, audit, complaint, and statutory reference (`EstablishedUnder`, `BenchOf`).

Three design rules govern the data layer, and they are what distinguish JEM from a directory.

**The primary-source rule.** Every field that moves a derived score or a gap marker is meant to carry a primary source: the Constitution, a central or state Act on india-code.nic.in, a gazette notification, a Supreme Court or High Court judgment, or an official report, each with an access date. Where a field cannot be confirmed from a primary source it is listed in `unverified_fields` and the entity's `data_quality` is capped at `partial`. Unverified entities render with a dashed border on the public map. The rule is enforced mechanically — the validation script rejects `data_quality: verified` without sources — and socially, through maintainer review of every merge. Nothing is filled by inference.

**Operational status as a first-class field.** Directories record that an institution exists in law. JEM records whether it exists in fact: `Active`, `Partial_Operational`, `Not_Constituted`, `Abolished`, `Merged`, `Suspended`. The distinction between a statute and a functioning bench is precisely the distinction pendency statistics cannot make, so the schema makes it unavoidable.

**Honest incompleteness.** The dataset carries a public gap registry — everything not yet covered, deferred, or blocked on external sources — and its numerics layer is deliberately sparse rather than deceptively full. In v1.0.0, 750 court and court-like entities carry a `judge_strength` block; only 44 have populated sanctioned-versus-working counts, because bulk population is blocked on Department of Justice vacancy reports and official rosters that we will transcribe but not invent. Similarly, 157 entities carry a `case_volume` block and 83 hold real figures from a prior National Judicial Data Grid merge; the newer state-pack courts have structural records only, because NJDG publishes no bulk, versioned district export suitable for reproducible research.² A structural claim in this paper rests on sourced fields; a numeric claim rests on the small verified subset; and the boundary between the two is visible in the data itself.

The pipeline is deliberately boring: YAML → schema validation → score derivation → compiled `graph.json` → a static web map, an API, and MCP tooling for agent-assisted research. Data is CC0, code is MIT, and the entire build is reproducible from the public repository. The live map at friedso.com/apps/jem is a showcase instance, not a canonical home — the design goal is that a university, newsroom, or think tank can stand up its own copy with its own coverage priorities.

A note on construction, disclosed as method rather than footnote: JEM was compiled through a human-supervised, AI-assisted workflow. Large language model agents (Anthropic's Claude, operating in research and data-entry sessions, and Cursor-based coding agents applying batch changes) drafted entity records and relationship edges against the schema; the maintainer commissioned each batch, resolved named decision gates before merge, and verified sources. The workflow's discipline is the point: agents were prohibited from inferring facts to fill gaps, every ambiguity was surfaced as an explicit decision gate rather than resolved by heuristic, and the validation pipeline ran on every change. Two findings below — the appellate rerouting in §III.4 and a modelling error the graph itself exposed³ — emerged directly from this process: the act of forcing scattered legal facts into a typed graph makes inconsistencies computable, and therefore findable.

## III. Four ways a forum fails

The typology below is not imposed on the data; it fell out of it. JEM's gap registry marks documented structural shortfalls per entity. Sorting those markers by mechanism, rather than by institution, yields four patterns. Each is presented here through its clearest documented case, with the primary notifications that anchor it.

### III.1 Forum delayed: GSTAT and the eight-year gap

The Goods and Services Tax Appellate Tribunal is the cleanest recent demonstration that a forum can exist in law long before it exists in fact — and of what the interval costs.

GSTAT was provided for in the Central Goods and Services Tax Act, 2017 (s.109). The constitution sequence, traced notification by notification: the Tribunal was established with effect from 1 September 2023, notified retrospectively through S.O. 3048(E) dated 31 July 2024; its first President, Justice (retd.) Sanjaya Kumar Mishra, was appointed in May 2024; the GSTAT (Procedure) Rules, 2025 were notified on 24 April 2025 (G.S.R. 256(E)); the tribunal and its e-filing portal launched in September 2025, with the window for filing legacy appeals extended to 30 June 2026; and 83 members — 34 technical, 49 judicial — were allotted to benches by Ministry of Finance office order on 26 December 2025, to join by 21 January 2026, with hearings phased through 2026. State benches are still being notified one at a time.

Roughly eight years, then, separate statutory creation from members seated for nationwide hearings. Note what the law does *not* contain: any statutory deadline requiring the tribunal to be operationalised. The CGST Act mandates the forum's existence and is silent on its birth date. That silence is a design fact, and it recurs across the tribunal landscape.

The appeals did not wait for the tribunal. With no statutory appellate forum below the High Courts, GST disputes travelled as Article 226 writ petitions — an appellate caseload absorbed by constitutional courts that were never resourced for it. The figure most often cited around the 2025 Procedure Rules puts the accumulated GST appeals at High Courts above eight thousand; we record that figure in the dataset as reported but unverified, because the underlying CBIC number has not been independently confirmed.⁴ What is verifiable is the doctrinal machinery of the transition now running in reverse: as GSTAT has come online, the Delhi and Telangana High Courts have begun relegating writ petitioners back to the tribunal on the ground that a statutory remedy now exists. The forum's absence redirected the flow upward for eight years; its arrival is redirecting the flow back down, unevenly, through transitional filing windows that track the phased rollout. Neither leg of that journey appears in any statistic as what it is.

### III.2 Forum never built: the unconstituted layer

If GSTAT is delay, nine bodies in JEM are something categorically different: legislated and never constituted at all. Two are national dispute-resolution institutions. The **Arbitration Council of India**, created by the 2019 amendment to the Arbitration and Conciliation Act, has never been constituted; the Supreme Court issued notice on the delay on 23 January 2025 — the constitutional court now adjudicating the executive's failure to build a body Parliament ordered seven years ago. The **Mediation Council of India** repeats the pattern on a fresher statute: the Mediation Act, 2023 mandates it, and three years on it does not exist. The remaining seven are state and Union Territory **Lokayuktas** — Arunachal Pradesh, Jammu & Kashmir, Meghalaya, Mizoram, Nagaland, Puducherry, and Sikkim — which means the anti-corruption ombudsman layer is simply missing across much of the Northeast, more than a decade after the Lokpal and Lokayuktas Act, 2013 required it.⁵

The distinction between this mode and the last is worth stating plainly, because the statistics erase it. A backlog is a queue: the forum exists and is slow. An unconstituted body is not a queue — it is a room the law promised and the administration never opened. There is no pendency figure for the Mediation Council because there is nothing to be pending before. The disputes it was designed to absorb are being absorbed elsewhere, invisibly, or not at all. JEM renders these entities with a distinct operational status and a dashed border so the difference between *slow* and *absent* is visible at a glance; the analytical point is that a research field organised around disposal rates has no native unit for *absent*.

### III.3 Forum hollowed: vacancy as a mode of operation

Delay is not only a birth defect of new tribunals. Established forums are hollowed from within, and the mechanism is the unfilled post.

The Appellate Tribunal for Electricity (APTEL) has heard appeals against central and state electricity regulators since 2005 under the Electricity Act, 2003 — on paper an active, two-decade-old central tribunal. In 2025 the Delhi High Court heard writ petitions arising from a prolonged vacancy in APTEL's Member (Technical) post, alongside a parallel vacancy at the Petroleum and Natural Gas Regulatory Board. The Court issued notice to the Union on 9 May 2025; the government subsequently informed the Court that the technical member had assumed office on 12 June 2025 — after judicial prompting — and the batch was disposed of on 24 July. The episode is small and precisely diagnostic: a statutory appointment machinery existed, the post sat empty for months anyway, and the mechanism that finally moved it was a writ petition. Staffing an appellate tribunal for the power sector is access-to-justice infrastructure for the industries that underwrite growth; in practice it required a constitutional court's attention to function.

The pattern generalises, and the dataset lets it be read across tiers rather than anecdotally. The Allahabad High Court — India's highest-volume High Court — carried roughly 80 vacancies against 160 sanctioned posts in a July 2025 snapshot: half its designed capacity. The National Green Tribunal sat below its statutory minimum on both member tracks in an August 2025 snapshot — six judicial and seven expert members against a statutory floor of ten each. The Securities Appellate Tribunal operates a single bench nationwide and went through a quorum crisis between December 2023 and April 2024. The Armed Forces Tribunal, whose orders can be challenged only by special leave in the Supreme Court — there is no High Court appellate layer — recorded fully vacant benches at Jabalpur, Guwahati, and Srinagar in a March 2026 snapshot. Each snapshot carries its source in the dataset. Each is, individually, a staffing story. Read together they describe an ecosystem in which "active" on a ministry website and *able to hear cases* are different facts, and only the first is routinely recorded.

### III.4 Forum ambiguous: the DRAT rerouting and litigation about where to litigate

The fourth mode is the least studied, and it is the one the graph representation is uniquely positioned to expose: the forum exists, is staffed, and litigants cannot reliably tell which one has jurisdiction — or which constitutional court supervises it.

The case study is the debt recovery stack. Debts Recovery Tribunals (DRTs) adjudicate bank recovery actions under the Recovery of Debts and Bankruptcy Act, 1993 and securitisation applications under the SARFAESI Act, 2002; appeals lie to five Debts Recovery Appellate Tribunals (DRATs), whose territorial jurisdiction over the thirty-nine DRTs is assigned not by statute but by executive notification. For the DRTs at Hyderabad and Visakhapatnam, that assignment has moved. G.S.R. 134(E) of 2000 placed them under DRAT Chennai. G.S.R. 538(E), dated 8 June 2018, reassigned both to **DRAT Kolkata** — where they remain, alongside the DRTs of Kolkata, Siliguri, Cuttack, and Guwahati.⁶ The reassignment was made without stated reasons in the notification itself, and it produced an appellate geography in which a borrower or bank in Visakhapatnam appeals an order not to Chennai, roughly 800 kilometres away, but to Kolkata, roughly double that — while the geographically and linguistically proximate DRAT continues to operate.

Distance is the visible cost. The structural cost is subtler and shows up one level higher. Orders of a DRAT are amenable to High Court supervision under Articles 226 and 227 — but *which* High Court, when the first-instance tribunal sits in Telangana or Andhra Pradesh and the appellate tribunal sits in West Bengal? The doctrinal test turns on the situs of the tribunal and the locus of the cause of action, a question the Supreme Court has confronted in the analogous context of tax tribunal benches hearing matters from other states,⁷ and it does not resolve cleanly here. In practice, both answers are being litigated. The Telangana High Court has entertained and decided writ petitions challenging orders of DRAT Kolkata arising from DRT-I Hyderabad — in one 2026 matter, dismissing a bank's challenge to the DRAT's refusal to condone a 224-day delay. The Calcutta High Court, in parallel lines of cases, has dealt with challenges to DRAT Kolkata orders in Hyderabad-origin matters while expressly noting that the action leading to the DRT's judgment arose beyond its territorial jurisdiction.⁸ Two constitutional courts, one appellate tribunal, and a preliminary question — *are you even the right court?* — that must be argued before any question of debt recovery is reached.

Call this **satellite litigation**: cases about where to litigate, generated not by the dispute but by the architecture. Every such threshold skirmish consumes constitutional-court time, adds a round of pleadings, and creates strategic option value for whichever party benefits from delay — an incentive structure that debt recovery law, of all fields, was specifically designed to eliminate. We are careful about what we claim here. JEM's case-volume layer does not yet hold the numbers to quantify how many writ petitions the ambiguity has generated or how much disposal time it has added; §V returns to this as a measurement agenda, not a finding. What the dataset does establish, with gazette-level sourcing, is the structure that makes the litigation possible: a single `AppealableTo` edge, reassigned by executive notification without stated criteria, whose supervisory shadow falls across two High Courts at once. In the graph this is not a narrative — it is a computable property. An edge whose source and target sit in different High Courts' territorial jurisdictions, under a supervisory doctrine keyed to territory, is a queryable pattern, and the debt recovery stack is unlikely to be its only instance.

It is worth recording that this finding was produced by the mapping exercise itself. The contested routing surfaced while wiring the DRT–DRAT appellate edges: the 2000 and 2018 notifications conflicted, the conflict forced a maintainer decision gate, and resolving the gate required tracing the gazette lineage. A prose description of the tribunal system can carry a contradiction indefinitely. A typed graph cannot — it validates or it does not — and that intolerance of ambiguity is the method's core research value.

## IV. Mechanism: independence as wiring

The typology describes where capacity fails. The dataset's relationship layer suggests *why* the failures persist, and the answer is uncomfortable in a specific, structural way: in a recurring set of cases, the institution responsible for building, staffing, or funding a forum is also a party whose interests the forum would constrain.

Independence is usually discussed as a matter of individual conduct — a judge's rectitude, a member's impartiality. Structurally, it is a matter of wiring: who appoints, who funds, who hears the complaint, who is the respondent. JEM traces all four chains per entity, and when the roles collapse into one body, the graph shows a loop. The Ministry of Finance appoints the members of CESTAT and controls its budget, while the Central Board of Indirect Taxes and Customs — an arm of the same ministry — is the respondent in effectively every appeal the tribunal hears. A complaint against a member of the NCLT goes to the Ministry of Corporate Affairs, the ministry that appointed the member. A state government appoints its electricity regulatory commission and owns the generation and distribution utility that commission polices; Tamil Nadu's TNERC–TANGEDCO pair is the documented instance in the dataset, and the structure is general. The RBI's internal ombudsman scheme routes appeals to a Deputy Governor of the RBI. In release v1.0.0, **187** bodies are flagged high or severe on structural independence risk.⁹

None of this is an accusation against any individual; it is a description of the diagram. But the diagram bears directly on the typology. If a fully operational tribunal would issue binding orders against a fund-constrained ministry — GSTAT against the revenue department, APTEL against a power ministry answerable for state utilities — then the ministry that controls the appointment calendar holds an instrument it need never visibly use. Slow constitution and tolerated vacancy are not necessarily strategic; administrative incapacity, coordination failure, and consultation bottlenecks are real and probably explain most of the record. The point the wiring makes is narrower and harder to dismiss: the system's design does not allow an observer to distinguish incapacity from strategy, because the actor with the motive also holds the mechanism, and no statutory clock forces its hand. A design that cannot demonstrate its own good faith is a design flaw independent of anyone's conduct.

JEM operationalises this layer through two derived, entity-level indicators: an independence-risk score built from the appointment, removal, funding, audit, and complaint-mechanism fields, and a discretionary-power score reflecting the reach and finality of an entity's adjudicatory authority. Both are algorithmic indicators of structural design — explicitly not findings of bias or misconduct — and both stay flagged wherever the underlying fields are unverified. Their construction and their limitations are discussed in §VI.

## V. Implications

**For measurement.** The immediate implication is a re-specification of what judicial statistics should count. Pendency is a flow measure over a forum set that is treated as fixed; the forum set is not fixed, and its perturbations — a tribunal unconstituted, a bench unfilled, a routing reassigned — are precisely the events that move the flow. A serious measurement programme would track, per forum: time from enactment to constitution; time from vacancy to appointment; and, for every appellate edge, whether its supervisory jurisdiction is territorially unambiguous. All three are structural variables, all three are derivable from public notifications, and none is currently published as a statistical series by any official body. JEM's schema holds fields for each; populating them at scale is the project's declared Phase 2, blocked today on the absence of bulk, versioned exports from the National Judicial Data Grid and the Department of Justice — itself a policy finding. The state publishes pendency, which describes the queue, and does not publish constitution lag or vacancy duration, which describe the state's own performance.

**For doctrine and drafting.** Two of the four failure modes are drafting artefacts. The *delayed* and *never built* modes share a root: Indian statutes that create adjudicatory bodies routinely omit any deadline for constituting them, and the omission converts a parliamentary mandate into an executive option. A single drafting convention — a statutory constitution clause with a default consequence, on the model of provisions that deem approvals granted after a lapse of time — would foreclose the entire category. The *ambiguous* mode is likewise a notification-hygiene problem: appellate reassignments made by executive order, without stated criteria and without addressing the supervisory-jurisdiction consequence, manufacture litigation. Requiring reassignment notifications to state their basis and to specify the supervising High Court would cost a paragraph of gazette text.

**For the spillover account of High Court pendency.** The four modes converge on one destination. GSTAT's missing bench became Article 226 petitions; APTEL's empty post became a writ; the unconstituted ACI became a Supreme Court notice; the DRAT ambiguity became parallel proceedings in two High Courts. The constitutional courts are the ecosystem's absorbing state — the forum of last resort not only doctrinally but hydraulically, receiving the overflow of every structural failure below. Some fraction of High Court pendency is therefore not High Court workload at all but displaced tribunal workload plus satellite litigation, and it is misdiagnosed when treated as a demand for more High Court judges rather than for functioning forums downstream. Estimating that fraction is, in our view, the single most valuable empirical project this dataset enables, and it requires exactly the marriage the dataset is built for: structural edges from JEM, case-level filings data from NJDG or High Court registries, and a difference design around constitution events — GSTAT's phased 2025–26 rollout is a natural experiment currently in progress.

**For research infrastructure.** Finally, an implication about method. The findings above were not produced by reading; they were produced by *modelling* — by the refusal of a typed schema to hold two contradictory gazette notifications, by graph-reference validation that surfaces unwired scaffold nodes, by validation that rejects a verified claim without a source. The dataset is CC0 and the pipeline is MIT precisely so that this discipline is inheritable: a state-level replication, a sectoral deep map of the tax adjudication chain, a fork tracking revenue courts, all start from the same schema. The showcase instance is not the point. The point is that structural literacy about the judiciary should be a public good with a version history.

## VI. Limitations

Four limitations bound the claims, and stating them precisely is part of the method.

**The scoring constructs are conflated, and we know it.** The current independence-risk and discretionary-power rubrics were built pragmatically, from the fields the schema captures. Measured against the comparative literature on institutional independence — Gilardi's index construction for regulatory agencies, Feld and Voigt's separation of de jure from de facto judicial independence, Hanretty and Koop's work on the dimensionality of formal independence, Maggetti's demonstration that de facto autonomy diverges from formal design — the current rubric conflates constructs that the literature keeps apart: formal independence (protection from external control) and discretionary power (the reach of what the body can decide) are distinct dimensions, and bundling financial and conduct accountability into one axis obscures both.¹⁰ A revised three-construct framework — separating formal independence, accountability completeness, and effective discretionary power, with the last computed by traversal of the graph's appellate edges rather than from entity-local fields — has been specified and is scheduled to replace the current rubric. Until it does, the published scores should be read as ordinal flags for structural attention, not as measures suitable for cross-entity regression. We publish them anyway, flagged, because a transparent imperfect indicator invites the correction a withheld one never receives.

**The numerics layer is thin, by design.** As §II records, only 44 of 750 judge-strength blocks and 83 of 157 case-volume blocks carry verified figures. Every quantitative claim in this paper rests either on a specifically sourced snapshot (the Allahabad vacancy count, the NGT member count, the GSTAT member allotment) or is explicitly marked unverified (the accumulated GST appeal figure). The alternative — imputing plausible numbers — would make the dataset more impressive and less trustworthy, and the project has chosen accordingly.

**No causal delay estimate is offered.** The DRAT case study documents an ambiguity and the litigation it permits; it does not measure the delay the ambiguity causes. The GSTAT case documents redirection; it does not decompose High Court GST pendency into displaced and organic components. Both estimates are feasible with filings-level data and are proposed, not performed.

**Coverage is uneven.** v1.0.0 covers all states and UTs at core-pack depth, with Tamil Nadu carrying the only full district lattice; district-level resolution elsewhere is Phase 3. Researchers should cite tagged releases, as this paper does, and treat the live map as a preview of the next version only when no tag is cited.

## VII. Open questions

The dataset was built to let these be asked with sources attached; it does not answer them.

Why do statutes creating adjudicatory bodies systematically omit constitution deadlines — drafting convention, or a preserved executive option? Across the tribunal population, does time-to-constitution correlate with the fiscal exposure of the parent ministry to the tribunal's likely orders — the strategic-deferral hypothesis — or with observable administrative covariates, which would vindicate the incapacity account? What fraction of High Court writ pendency in tax, electricity, and debt recovery is displaced tribunal workload, and how fast does it drain when the forum finally opens — a question GSTAT's ongoing rollout will answer for anyone positioned to measure it? How many other `AppealableTo` edges in the Indian lattice cross High Court territorial boundaries the way the Hyderabad–Kolkata edge does, and what does the full set of ambiguous supervisory shadows look like when computed rather than encountered case by case?

And beneath all of these, the question the map keeps returning: a queue can be shortened by working faster. What is the remedy when the room was never opened, the referee reports to a player, and the sign on the door points two directions at once? The pendency numbers will not say. That is why they are the second question.
